Chronology

A Life as a Sequence of Orbits

  • BEFORE 1982 — THE UNWRITTEN FIELD
    Pre-birth memory: patterns without a storyteller

    Before any first breath, there is already movement: heartbeats, voices, vibrations, hormones, gravity, light soaking through skin. The nervous system listens long before it can speak. These are proto-memories — not “stories” but tendencies. In Funebra language: 0D proto-bn, tiny potential points waiting for coordinates.

  • 1982–1985 — ARRIVAL TO THE 1D/2D WORLD
    The body learns first; language comes later

    The early years are made of milk, sound and gravity. Motor patterns crystallize: reaching, falling, walking, dancing. The body encodes a massive library of “what happens if I move like this?” This is procedural memory, the oldest intelligence. Funebra calls this the perfect movement law: reality rewards the movements that keep the orbit stable.

  • MID-1980s → EARLY-1990s — EMOTIONAL GEOMETRY
    The world splits into safe / unsafe constellations

    Faces become constellations. Rooms become weather. A voice can be home or storm. Emotional memory maps are drawn in invisible ink, but they guide everything: who you run to, who you hide from, what feels like “too much”. Each strong feeling is a bn-point: a node where body, place and meaning intersect.

  • AROUND AGE 3 — THE TIMELINE SWITCH TURNS ON
    “That happened to me” – the birth of a navigator

    At some point, the hippocampus is ready, and something subtle happens: experiences stop being isolated flashes and begin to line up. For the first time you can say “I remember when…”. This is the birth of the 4D self — a being that can walk not only in space, but in time, revisiting old rooms in the mind.

  • 1990s–2000s — MEMORY TURNS INTO AN OPERATING SYSTEM
    If / then / else: life starts compiling

    School, friendships, first loves, first losses. The brain learns to run code: if I say this, then they react like that, else I stay silent. Decisions become scripts. Repeated scripts become identity. The universe now runs nested conditionals inside a human mind, like a cosmic computer experimenting with itself.

  • 2010s–2020s — HYBRID AGE
    When silicon starts remembering with you

    Phones, feeds, clouds, AI. Memory is no longer just in neurons — it spills into servers and networks. Photos, chats and songs become external hippocampi. You scroll your own past like a timeline you can drag with a finger. The border between “my memory” and “my archive” starts to blur. Funebra names this Hybrid Advanced Intelligence: biological time and digital time running in parallel, cross-syncing.

  • 2025 → 2050 — THE OPEN FUTURE
    Memory as a launchpad, not a prison

    From the present to 2050, nothing is written — but much is already prepared. Every memory is a vector, a direction that feels more “natural” to follow. Time travel here is not about machines; it is about editing which memories you orbit. In 2050, the body is older, but if the orbit is conscious, the intelligence is younger: more curious, less afraid.

Philosophical View · Why Memory Feels Like Time Travel

When you remember something vividly, you do not see a photograph — you re-enter a coordinate system. The light, the temperature, the emotion, the options you believed you had at that time: they all reconstruct themselves around you. The nervous system briefly abandons “now” and bootloads “then”.

From a Funebra perspective, a memory is not a frozen picture; it is a compressed movement pattern. To recall it is to let that pattern decompress and replay. For a few seconds, the orbit of your consciousness leaves 2025 and flows along 1996, 2003 or 1982 again. No external time machine needed — the traveller is the pattern.

Practical View · How to Travel Intentionally

You cannot change what happened in 1982, but you can change which part of 1982 you visit. Most people orbit pain by default — their time travel is involuntary. Funebra suggests a different protocol:

1. Choose a memory that contains both pain and wisdom.
2. Re-enter it slowly: breath, posture, environment.
3. Ask: “What did this version of me need to know?”
4. Bring that answer back as a gift to the present.

Now you are not trapped in the past; you are harvesting it. This is time travel as alchemy, not escape.

“Memory is not a warehouse of dead moments. It is a living geometry that allows consciousness to bend its own timeline. When you remember with awareness, you are not going backwards — you are folding time so that past and present can speak to each other.”
